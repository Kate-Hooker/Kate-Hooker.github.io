Identity ⭒ Values ⭒ Strength

Coming from a frugal hardworking background most of my values are drawn from treasuring new opportunities, inner strength and constant personal growth. I’m a minimalist - filtering most of my spare income into projects, experiments and experiences, enriching my lifes’ tapestry with the goal of evolving constantly instead of simply existing to buy new things.

I particularly value creative thinkers - I think the world is saturated with amazing tools that allow people to expand their minds and it absolutely flaws me that people just dont use them.

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision?

I recently made the ethical decision not to go to a wedding and I still feel bad about it.

As a general rule I don’t enjoy weddings - they’re a little archaic (giving away virgins etc) and a little bit culty, and a little bit sad that people have all pressure on them to drop a house deposit or a college fund on a big spangley party - also no disrespect to people that like them - its a nice party - I get it - they’re just not for me.

Anyway, I finally had one I actually wanted to go to - my ethical dilemma arose after I had accepted the invitation when they began advertising that there would be a second open bar available throughout the night serving pharmaceuticals and uppers, and I’m a full on sober t-totaller - not even caffeine, barely even sugar - sort of person.

Having quit drinking a long time ago I don’t mind being around people that are having fun but substance abuse (particularly at the level being encouraged here) just feels quite monstrous to me and makes people really hard to talk to.

I weighed up going in early and just staying for the start of it but I would have felt so rude leaving an hour in because of sobriety - I didn’t want to seem judgemental or feel out of place because it’s supposed to be a big free celebration of love.

I weighed it up ethically and socially, reneged on the invitation and didn’t go.

I feel bad, but I think it was better for everyone.

Was I right? I don’t know.

Describe your culture and how the people around you have influenced your value and identity

Like many people growing up in smalltown 90s New Zealand my hometown became the blue print for the lifestyle I never wanted to live, I used university as my excuse and got out fast.

I came straight to Wellington and started at Vic and got (most) of an arts degree (Film and Psychology) and all of a film diploma (Yes, very funny)

And did what anyone with a BA in people watching would do and started running bars in lieu of a real job. For 15 years.

I did well in the industry because I was reliable, pragmatic, quick on my feet, good at fixing things, good at managing people, and socially connected to a point I could get most issues sorted before anybody else noticed there was a problem. I did well and had some great mentors.

This industry taught me to value grit and humility above all. My values are firmly grounded in the realm of hardworking, down to earth, good people.

I like problem solvers who have worked hard to get where they are, people that hunger to stay relevant and better themselves, and developed the key goal in life to remain hungry for development and betterment.

also learnt a special respect for creatives that have learnt to apply their rampant creativity to a structure, make it marketable and monetize it.

Identify your strengths that will support your learning journey?

I’m driven, stubborn and competitive - I don’t mind competing with other people but I generally just compete with myself.

Beat my last score - take better notes this time - make a road map to solve this so next time I can get straight through it etc.

I love jedi mind tricks and bio hacks for learning - I fully believe we can expand our comprehension and neuroplasticity by training our minds - I think it’s genuinely advantageous and I love using myself as a test subject.

I’m very curious and creative - I love harnessing my adhd and using it to my advantage. I can throw out a lot of solutions to a problem I understand properly and take criticism really well (because I know full well that 9 out of those 10 solutions will be bad and thats all part of the process)

I get equal satisfaction solving other peoples problems - I really like collaborating, even if its just being a sounding board. I think its a really special thing to help bump someone else onto the right path when they’re a bit stuck.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, your reflections on on any other strategies you would try now and why?

I was having some struggles at a job (office job, court clerk for the government, admin) because I was finding that the space was much too disruptive for me.

I think the tension arrose because my way of dealing with discomfort is blocking it out and ignoring it. My desk placement was opposite a person with a compulsive singing issue - when they had their airpods in and were in flow they would compulsively (badly) sing (/moan, hum, singing is a stretch, there weren't many words) along with anything they listened to, despite an office full of people asking them not to. In the darker hours other workmates would start to give in and join in bad sing-alongs to try lift the mood and the singer was very liked, and hardworking, so no negative comments were made. I think it might have been a comulsion tick so I didn't bring it up either.

I can’t concentrate on work while there’s singing, I really really started to dislike it but I needed to stay seated with my team because I was still in training and they were the people I needed to help me learn. I tried listening to music but that would zone me out too much, and I felt like I was missing out on the social aspect of the office because it cut me off from all conversation.

I tried timing my day so I wouldn’t overlap shifts and breaks with them, which helped a little, but in the end I resorted to just loading my phone with podcasts and sitting listening to those all afternoon to block out the ruckus. And while it may have made me seem a bit isolated from the team I’m 100% sure it kept me saner and much more productive.

In hindsight I probably should have just asked them nicely to shut up, and asked to be moved somewhere quieter.

But its hard to do that in a big environment where this behaviour is an established norm - nobody wants to be the fun police, and they were just young and trying to make the most of a crappy office.
