⭒ Neuroplasticity & Growth Mindset ⭒

Neuroplasticity is the acknowledgement that our brains are eternally malleable, but we have to choose whether or not we unlock this.

It helps us realise that it takes time to train, redirect and reinforce our neurons but if we can maintain a pattern of proactive study we can develop our minds in powerful ways.

It also acknowledges that neuroplasticity can be influenced by much more than what we think of as classic book learning and intelligence - it is shaped by our thoughts, environment and lifestyle.

On a physical level our neural pathways are like a huge twisting mass of roads and highways that information can travel down - as we start to learn new skills new paths are forged - delicate and breakable at first, but the more we use them the more the brain and body realises to reinforce them.

If we train our brain to know that every day we learn, and practise learning, it prepares itself to wire and strengthen those pathways faster but for this to happen practise, learning and self extension must be happening daily in our lives.

This is also why tests and reflections on our study materials are highly effective learning tools because they force us to review our own learning and re-ignite old neural pathways - re-strengthening them, but also building our awareness of our own abilities and knowledge to help promote dopamine release as a learning reward.

Unfortunately this practise and repetition model can also work to our detriment when we fall into negative patterns, If we teach ourselves that all we want to do is lie down and watch netflix every day and memorise the spiralling details of some TV epic instead of practising an instrument or trying new things our brain will reinforce those pathways instead.

Negative self reinforcement can greatly hamper our potential if we allow the wrong pathways to be reinforced - part of training ourselves to learn every day is convincing our brains to fire off their chemical reward systems to keep us loving life and learning. If we accidently condition ourselves to believe we have limitations where we don’t, those pathways become reinforced and those limitations become genuine - if we tell ourselves we cannot learn, we will not try, and we will not learn - and everytime that pathway becomes reinforced our self inflicted barriers become denser and stronger and harder to break.

The mind has amazing abilities to limit what we can and cannot do - did you know when people are in comas they can do the splits? We are all completely capable of the flexibility required to make it safely achievable but our brains have a designated stop-point that will freeze our muscle at a certain point in a stretch when we hit our perceived flexibility limited. In order to learn how to do it we have to slowly consistently move our muscles to that edge and gently relax into stretching further and further each time so our brain agrees to withdraws that barrier and allow us to perform at our full potential.

This mindset effect makes understanding the principals of neuroplasticity even more important - people that understand that they are innately capable of learning and fostering their own high level intelligence leaves them open and able to do so.

This growth mindset where we practise having an open attitude to learning becomes particularly powerful as we evolve to treat learning as a journey, and accept that the most rewarding journeys are never the easiest ones. This mindset teaches that pitfalls, failures and challenges are all opportunities for further development and growth. The harder we work for success the bigger the dopamine hit at the end, and the more our human capabilities expand. It reminds us that effort is necessary for later reward, and critiques of our work are only meant to be helpful. We must reframe challenge, and not let anything hold back our growth - especially if it is just poor thinking practise.

So knowing all this what are some easy ways to incorporate this into our learning practises to make our learning journeys more efficient and productive?

As we push ourselves struggles and challenges are inevitable - we must reframe perceived ‘failures’ and see them as learning opportunities and reasons to seek help from experts and delve deeper.

Associating guilt and failure with learning is a really bad practise. This is where a good understanding of mindfulness will come in handy (fielding those negative thoughts and sorting out the ones that serve no good purpose) and is also where a learning journal can be really helpful - it tracks incremental success and gives a really nice frame of reference to those days where you just feel like you’re going nowhere.

Using whatever access we have to our learning peers, and building lines of communication with connections that have been through similar training can also be greatly beneficial here - everybody has different strategies for coping with this feeling and inevitabley reaching out for help, support or advice will help you access these resources.

Learning journals are also great for nailing down old techniques that have worked in the past and help you revisit them when your stuck.

There are plenty of ways to hack neuroplasticity once you get into the science behind it - experimenting with a variety of learning techniques is a really good one (essays, podcasts, videos, talking about the subject live) because as soon as one begins to feel cumbersome or perplexing you can switch to a new learning medium and reinforce a slightly different pathway without risking andy negative reinforcement and still developing your learning and growth.

I’m a big fan of brain boosting nootropics - go for anything that boosts brain cell growth and memory. I practise a variety of mindfulness activities - yoga meditation, Wim Hoff, all that good stuff, take breaks, live a very low toxin life to keep my synapses in good shape and I only watch trash on the internet for truly medicinal purposes.

Because everybody needs a little lazy dopamine mining system somewhere.

Staying stimulated is key. Use it or lose it.

Learning everyday or practising something that powers your brain up is key.

We have the ability to never stop evolving - why wouldn't we?
